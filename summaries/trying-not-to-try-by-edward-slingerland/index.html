Trying Not to Try by Edward Slingerland (Summary & Review)

author Jared Wright

“Trying Not to Try: Ancient China, Modern Science, and the Power of Spontaneity” by Edward Slingerland is a fascinating exploration of the Chinese concept of wu-wei and its relevance to modern life. Slingerland is a professor of Asian Studies at the University of British Columbia, and his book draws on a range of sources, from ancient Chinese texts to contemporary cognitive psychology, to explore the nature of spontaneous action.

The book argues that we often try too hard to achieve our goals, and that this can actually hinder our ability to succeed. Drawing on insights from the Chinese philosophers Laozi and Zhuangzi, Slingerland suggests that we can learn to cultivate a state of relaxed attentiveness that allows us to act in a more spontaneous and effective way.

He explores how this concept is supported by recent findings in neuroscience and cognitive psychology, which have demonstrated the importance of the unconscious mind in decision-making and action.

Generally speaking, “Trying Not to Try” is a thought-provoking and engaging exploration of a concept that is central to many Chinese philosophical traditions. It offers a compelling argument for the value of spontaneity and non-striving, and provides practical advice for cultivating these qualities in our own lives. If you’re interested in philosophy, psychology, or self-improvement, this book is well worth reading.

Idea 1: Wu-wei, a central concept in ancient Chinese philosophy, refers to a state of effortless action or non-striving.

Idea 2: Trying too hard can actually hinder our ability to achieve our goals by creating anxiety and overthinking.

Idea 3: The key to wu-wei is to cultivate a state of relaxed attentiveness that allows us to act spontaneously and effectively.

Idea 4: This state of relaxed attentiveness is supported by recent findings in neuroscience and cognitive psychology that have demonstrated the importance of the unconscious mind in decision-making and action.

Idea 5: The Chinese philosophers Laozi and Zhuangzi saw wu-wei as a way to align with the natural order of the universe and achieve harmony.

Idea 6: Confucianism, on the other hand, emphasized the importance of effort and striving in achieving moral excellence and social harmony.

Idea 7: The tension between wu-wei and Confucianism reflects a broader tension in human nature between the spontaneous and the deliberate.

Idea 8: Cultivating wu-wei requires a shift in our mindset from one of trying to one of letting go, and learning to trust our instincts and intuition.

Idea 9: Practices such as meditation, mindfulness, and flow states can help us cultivate the state of relaxed attentiveness needed for wu-wei.

Idea 10: The principles of wu-wei have practical applications in areas such as leadership, creativity, and relationships, and can help us live a more fulfilling and authentic life.
