Trying Not to Try by Edward Slingerland (Summary & Review)

author Jared Wright
Rating

“Trying Not to Try: Ancient China, Modern Science, and the Power of Spontaneity” is a book written by Edward Slingerland that explores the concept of wu-wei, a central idea in ancient Chinese philosophy. The book draws upon both traditional Chinese philosophy and modern scientific research to provide insights into the power of spontaneity and effortless action.

Slingerland’s main intention in writing this book is to bridge the gap between ancient Chinese wisdom and contemporary Western thought. He aims to demonstrate that the concepts and practices developed by ancient Chinese philosophers are not only relevant but also supported by modern scientific findings.

The book examines the paradoxical idea of wu-wei, which can be translated as “non-action” or “effortless action.” It delves into the notion that by not trying too hard and allowing oneself to go with the flow, individuals can achieve better results and lead more fulfilling lives. Slingerland explores how this concept can be applied in various aspects of life, including personal relationships, creativity, leadership, and decision-making.

Idea 1: The Paradoxical Nature of Wu-Wei

In his exploration of the concept of Wu-Wei, Slingerland delves into its paradoxical nature. On the surface, Wu-Wei seems counterintuitive, as it suggests that by doing less and relinquishing control, individuals can achieve better outcomes. This challenges our conventional understanding of action and effort, which often emphasizes striving, control, and exerting one’s will.

However, Slingerland argues that Wu-Wei is not about inaction or laziness but rather a dynamic engagement with the world that is free from the constraints of excessive control and forced effort. It is a state of being in harmony with the natural flow of events, where actions arise spontaneously and effortlessly.

The author highlights the balance between intention and spontaneity that is central to Wu-Wei. It is not a passive state of resignation, but an active and mindful way of engaging with the world. By observing and understanding the natural rhythms and patterns of the world, individuals can align their actions with these currents, allowing things to unfold naturally.

Slingerland encourages readers to embrace the power of Wu-Wei by letting go of the need to control every aspect of their lives. He suggests that by cultivating self-awareness, practicing mindfulness, and developing an intuitive understanding of the world, individuals can navigate through life with greater ease and effectiveness. Wu-Wei becomes a guiding principle for living a more authentic, balanced, and fulfilling life.

Idea 2: The Role of Rituals and Practices

Ancient Chinese thinkers recognized the role of rituals and practices in cultivating the state of Wu-Wei. Slingerland delves into the significance of these practices, shedding light on how they enable individuals to quiet the mind, connect with their inner selves, and tap into the natural flow of existence.

Meditation and mindfulness practices, for example, are highlighted as essential tools for developing self-awareness and cultivating a state of presence. By training the mind to observe thoughts and emotions without judgment, individuals can detach from the constant mental chatter and immerse themselves fully in the present moment.

Scientific evidence is presented to support the benefits of these practices. Research has shown that regular meditation and mindfulness can enhance cognitive function, improve emotional well-being, reduce stress, and increase resilience. These practices help individuals become more attuned to their own internal states and the external environment, facilitating the emergence of Wu-Wei in daily life.

Slingerland also explores other rituals and practices, such as tai chi, qigong, and yoga, which are designed to cultivate a harmonious balance between the body, mind, and spirit. These practices promote physical health, mental clarity, and energetic alignment, enabling individuals to access states of flow and effortless action.

The book emphasizes the transformative power of incorporating these rituals and practices into daily life. By making them a part of one’s routine, individuals can create a foundation for Wu-Wei to flourish, ultimately leading to a more fulfilling and purposeful existence.

Idea 3: The Power of Flow States

Flow states, characterized by complete immersion and focus in an activity, are integral to the concept of Wu-Wei. Slingerland explores the neuroscience behind flow states and their connection to effortless action.

He delves into the conditions that facilitate the occurrence of flow states, such as clear goals, immediate feedback, and a balance between skill and challenge. When these conditions are met, individuals can enter a state of flow where time seems to dissolve, self-consciousness fades away, and actions unfold effortlessly.

Slingerland provides practical strategies for individuals to enter and maintain flow states in their daily lives. He suggests identifying activities that bring joy, challenge, and a sense of purpose. By engaging in these activities, individuals can tap into their innate talents and passions, increasing the likelihood of experiencing flow.

The author also explores the role of creativity in flow states. He discusses how the convergence of skill, challenge, and spontaneity can lead to creative breakthroughs and innovations. By letting go of conscious effort and tapping into the unconscious mind, individuals can access new insights and solutions to problems.

Idea 4: The Art of Effortless Relationships

The principles of Wu-Wei can be applied to personal relationships, fostering harmony and connection. Slingerland explores how letting go of control and embracing spontaneity can lead to more fulfilling and authentic relationships.

The book emphasizes the importance of empathy, active listening, and non-verbal communication in cultivating effortless relationships. By truly understanding and resonating with others, individuals can respond intuitively and spontaneously, creating a space of trust and openness.

Slingerland suggests that being present in the moment is crucial for effortless relationships. By letting go of preconceived judgments and expectations, individuals can fully engage with others and respond authentically to the dynamics of the interaction. This requires relinquishing the need to control or manipulate outcomes and instead allowing the relationship to unfold naturally.

The author also discusses the role of non-verbal communication in effortless relationships. He explores body language, facial expressions, and other non-verbal cues that convey empathy, understanding, and connection. By paying attention to these subtle signals, individuals can deepen their relationships and create an environment of mutual support and growth.

The book offers practical insights and techniques for applying the principles of Wu-Wei to relationships. It encourages individuals to cultivate self-awareness, practice empathy, and develop a deep sense of presence in their interactions with others. By doing so, they can foster effortless relationships that nourish both themselves and those around them.

Idea 5: Creativity and Wu-Wei

Slingerland explores the relationship between Wu-Wei and creativity, highlighting the role of spontaneity in the creative process. He argues that by letting go of conscious effort and tapping into the unconscious mind, individuals can unlock their creative potential.

The book delves into the idea that creativity emerges from a state of flow and presence. When individuals are fully engaged in an activity, immersed in the present moment, and detached from self-consciousness, they can access new perspectives, ideas, and solutions.

Slingerland presents practical techniques for enhancing creative thinking and problem-solving by embracing Wu-Wei. He suggests practices such as free writing, brainstorming without judgment, and engaging in activities that stimulate the imagination. These techniques encourage individuals to bypass self-imposed limitations and allow their creative instincts to guide them.

The author also explores the concept of “creative incubation,” where individuals consciously engage with a problem or project and then let go of conscious effort, allowing the unconscious mind to work on it. By relinquishing control and giving the mind space to make connections and generate insights, individuals can tap into their creative wellspring.

The book encourages readers to embrace the paradoxical nature of creativity, where effort and non-effort coexist. It highlights the importance of balancing focused effort with moments of relaxation and receptivity, creating an environment conducive to creative inspiration and breakthroughs.

Idea 6: Leadership and the Art of Non-Interference

Drawing from ancient Chinese wisdom, Slingerland discusses a different approach to leadership based on non-interference and allowing subordinates to flourish. He challenges conventional Western leadership practices that emphasize control and micromanagement.

The book explores the concept of “leading without leading,” where leaders create an environment of trust and empowerment, allowing individuals to act autonomously and contribute their unique talents and insights. By relinquishing excessive control and fostering a culture of collaboration and shared responsibility, leaders can unleash the full potential of their teams.

Slingerland emphasizes the importance of empathy and active listening in leadership. By truly understanding the needs and aspirations of team members, leaders can provide the necessary support and guidance without imposing their own agendas. This requires letting go of ego-driven decision-making and embracing a more holistic and inclusive approach.

The author provides practical strategies for leaders to cultivate the art of non-interference. This includes delegating authority, providing clear goals and expectations, and creating space for innovation and experimentation. By trusting in the abilities of their team members and fostering an environment of psychological safety, leaders can nurture a culture of Wu-Wei that leads to greater creativity, productivity, and satisfaction.

Idea 7: Decision-Making and Intuition

Traditional Chinese philosophy places a strong emphasis on intuition and trusting one’s instincts. Slingerland explores how Wu-Wei can influence decision-making by guiding individuals to tap into their intuitive wisdom.

The book challenges the prevailing belief that rational analysis alone leads to the best decisions. It argues that by combining rationality with intuitive insights, individuals can access a broader range of information and make more informed choices.

Slingerland presents scientific evidence that supports the role of intuition in decision-making. He highlights research showing that the unconscious mind can process and integrate vast amounts of information, leading to intuitive hunches and gut feelings that are often accurate and reliable.

The book provides practical techniques for honing intuition and incorporating it into the decision-making process. This includes practices such as mindfulness, meditation, and reflective journaling, which help individuals develop self-awareness and access their intuitive wisdom.

By embracing Wu-Wei in decision-making, individuals can let go of excessive analysis and trust their inner guidance. This allows for more agile and adaptive decision-making, particularly in complex and uncertain situations where rationality alone may fall short.

Idea 8: Effortless Action in a Modern World

Slingerland addresses the challenges of applying the principles of Wu-Wei in a modern, fast-paced society. He acknowledges that finding moments of stillness and cultivating spontaneity can be difficult amidst the demands and distractions of daily life.

The book provides practical strategies for integrating Wu-Wei into the modern world. It suggests carving out intentional periods of silence and solitude, engaging in mindful activities such as walking or gardening, and creating a physical and mental environment that supports effortless action.

Slingerland also explores the role of technology in our lives and its impact on our ability to cultivate Wu-Wei. He encourages individuals to be mindful of their relationship with technology, seeking a balance between its benefits and the need for disconnecting and recharging.

The author highlights the importance of self-care and stress management in cultivating Wu-Wei. He suggests practices such as exercise, adequate sleep, and healthy eating as essential for maintaining physical and mental well-being. By taking care of themselves, individuals can create a solid foundation for effortless action in their daily lives.

The book emphasizes that integrating Wu-Wei into a modern lifestyle requires conscious effort and commitment. It encourages readers to start small, gradually incorporating moments of stillness and spontaneity into their routines. Over time, these practices can become natural and effortless, leading to a more balanced and fulfilling life.

Idea 9: The Limitations of Wu-Wei

While extolling the virtues of Wu-Wei, Slingerland acknowledges its limitations. He cautions against the misinterpretation of Wu-Wei as passivity or laziness. The book emphasizes the importance of discerning when to act and when to let go, offering a nuanced understanding of the concept.

Slingerland explores situations where effort and active intervention are necessary. He highlights the importance of discernment and understanding the context in which Wu-Wei is applied. While Wu-Wei encourages individuals to embrace spontaneity and relinquish control, it does not negate the need for deliberate action when circumstances require it.

The author suggests that Wu-Wei is not a one-size-fits-all approach but a guiding principle that requires adaptability and discernment. Different situations may call for different approaches, and individuals must develop the wisdom to know when to engage and when to let go.

The book encourages readers to cultivate self-awareness and a deep understanding of their own tendencies and inclinations. By developing this self-knowledge, individuals can navigate the complexities of life and make informed decisions about when to apply Wu-Wei and when to take deliberate action.

Idea 10: The Relevance of Wu-Wei in Modern Science

Slingerland draws connections between ancient Chinese philosophy and modern scientific research, showcasing the convergence of ideas. He demonstrates how contemporary studies in cognitive science, neuroscience, and psychology align with the principles of Wu-Wei.

The book highlights scientific evidence that supports the benefits of practices such as meditation, mindfulness, and flow states, which are integral to Wu-Wei. Research has shown that these practices enhance cognitive function, emotional well-being, and performance.

Slingerland also explores the concept of “embodied cognition” and its connection to Wu-Wei. This idea suggests that cognition is not solely a function of the brain but is influenced by the body and its interactions with the environment. By cultivating embodied awareness, individuals can access intuitive knowledge and engage in more effortless and effective actions.

The author argues that the principles of Wu-Wei are relevant across cultures and time periods. While rooted in ancient Chinese philosophy, they align with universal principles of human psychology and behavior. By bridging the gap between East and West, the book showcases the universal relevance of Wu-Wei and its potential to enhance well-being and performance in the modern world.

Who is this Book Recommended For?

“Trying Not to Try” is recommended for individuals interested in personal growth, Eastern philosophy, and the intersection of ancient wisdom with modern science. It appeals to readers who are open to exploring alternative approaches to success and happiness. The book provides practical insights and exercises that can benefit anyone seeking to improve their relationships, boost creativity, enhance decision-making, or develop effective leadership skills. Additionally, those interested in mindfulness, meditation, and flow states will find valuable insights in this book.

Review of the Book

“Trying Not to Try” succeeds in bridging the gap between ancient Chinese philosophy and contemporary Western thought. Edward Slingerland presents a compelling argument for the relevance of Wu-Wei in our modern lives, supported by both traditional wisdom and scientific research. The book effectively combines engaging storytelling, scholarly analysis, and practical exercises to provide a comprehensive exploration of the concept of effortless action.

Slingerland skillfully weaves together anecdotes, historical examples, and scientific evidence to make the concepts relatable and understandable. The inclusion of practical exercises and strategies encourages readers to actively apply the principles of Wu-Wei in their lives, enhancing the book’s practical value.

Keep in mind that some readers may find the book overly focused on the philosophical and historical aspects, which could detract from its practical application for a modern audience.

The dense academic references and extensive discussions of ancient Chinese texts may be less engaging for those seeking a more concise and straightforward self-help book. Additionally, while the book acknowledges the limitations of Wu-Wei, a more in-depth exploration of potential challenges and how to address them could have been beneficial.

But in general, “Trying Not to Try” is a book that offers valuable insights into the power of spontaneity and effortless action. It successfully brings together ancient wisdom and modern science, providing readers with a compelling framework for enhancing various aspects of their lives. By exploring the paradoxes of Wu-Wei and its practical applications, the book offers a fresh perspective on success, well-being, and living a fulfilling life.

Conclusion

“Trying Not to Try: Ancient China, Modern Science, and the Power of Spontaneity” by Edward Slingerland is a captivating exploration of the concept of wu-wei and its relevance in today’s world. The book highlights ten key ideas, including the paradoxical nature of Wu-Wei, the role of rituals and practices, the power of flow states, effortless relationships, creativity, leadership, decision-making, and the challenges of applying Wu-Wei in a modern context.

Recommended for those interested in personal growth, Eastern philosophy, and the integration of ancient wisdom with modern science, the book provides practical insights and exercises for applying the principles of Wu-Wei in daily life.

While it may be less appealing to readers seeking a strictly self-help book or a concise exploration, “Trying Not to Try” offers a comprehensive and well-researched examination of the power of spontaneity and effortless action.

It encourages readers to embrace the wisdom of ancient China while acknowledging the limitations and complexities of applying Wu-Wei in a modern society.

The book leaves readers with a deeper understanding of how to cultivate spontaneity and find greater fulfillment in their lives.
