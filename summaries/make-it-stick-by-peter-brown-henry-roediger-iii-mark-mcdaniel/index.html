Make It Stick by Peter Brown (Summary & Review)

author Jared Wright
Rating

“Make It Stick: The Science of Successful Learning” by Peter Brown, Henry Roediger III, and Mark McDaniel is a mind-stretching book that explores the science behind effective learning and provides practical strategies to enhance learning and retention.

The authors aim to challenge common misconceptions about learning and offer evidence-based techniques that have been proven to enhance long-term retention and application of knowledge.

The book emphasizes the importance of active learning, retrieval practice, and the deliberate use of spacing and interleaving techniques. It presents research findings from cognitive psychology and neuroscience to explain how the human brain learns and retains information most effectively.

The authors also share real-world examples and anecdotes to illustrate the concepts and provide practical guidance for implementing these techniques in various learning contexts.

“Make It Stick” seeks to empower learners and educators by providing evidence-based strategies that challenge traditional study methods and promote deeper understanding and long-term retention of knowledge.

By debunking common myths about learning and presenting alternative approaches rooted in scientific research, the book strives to revolutionize the way people approach learning and teaching.

Idea 1: Embrace the Power of Retrieval Practice

Retrieval practice refers to the act of actively recalling information from memory, which has been shown to strengthen memory retention. Rather than passively reviewing material, engaging in active retrieval practice enhances long-term learning. When you actively try to recall information, you reinforce the neural pathways associated with that knowledge, making it easier to retrieve in the future.

To make the most of retrieval practice, actively quiz yourself on the material you’re learning. This could involve using flashcards, summarizing key concepts without referring to your notes, or engaging in group discussions where you take turns recalling and explaining information. By repeatedly retrieving information from memory, you strengthen your ability to retain and apply that knowledge.

Example: When studying a new language, instead of merely reviewing vocabulary lists, quiz yourself by trying to recall the meanings of words or constructing sentences using the new words you’ve learned.

Practical exercise: Take a short break from your current study session, then try to recall the main points or concepts you covered during that session. This brief retrieval practice will help solidify your learning and reinforce your memory of the material.

Idea 2: Emphasize Interleaving for Better Learning

Interleaving involves mixing different topics or types of problems during practice sessions rather than focusing on a single skill or topic at a time. This technique promotes greater cognitive flexibility and the ability to transfer knowledge across different contexts.

While interleaving may initially feel more challenging, it leads to deeper understanding and enhances long-term retention compared to block practice, where you focus on a single topic or skill for an extended period.

By practicing in an interleaved manner, you create connections between related but distinct concepts, fostering a more robust and flexible knowledge base. This approach encourages your brain to engage in active processing and problem-solving, which contributes to enhanced learning and retrieval.

Example: Instead of exclusively practicing one type of math problem, mix different types of problems, such as algebraic equations, geometry proofs, and word problems. This forces you to think critically and apply different problem-solving strategies.

Practical exercise: When studying multiple subjects or topics, interleave your study sessions by alternating between subjects rather than dedicating large blocks of time to each subject separately. This practice enhances your ability to recall and connect information across different domains.

Idea 3: Embrace Desirable Difficulties

Contrary to popular belief, making learning too easy can hinder long-term retention. Desirable difficulties refer to the deliberate introduction of challenges or obstacles during the learning process. When you encounter difficulties while learning, it forces you to engage in deeper processing and promotes more robust memory formation.

By intentionally spacing your learning sessions, introducing retrieval practice, and interleaving topics, you create the necessary challenges for desirable difficulties. These challenges prompt your brain to work harder and develop stronger neural connections, leading to improved long-term learning outcomes.

Example: When learning to play a musical instrument, deliberately practice difficult passages or complex techniques instead of solely focusing on familiar and comfortable exercises. The challenging practice will lead to greater skill development and retention.

Practical exercise: Identify the most challenging aspects of the subject or skill you are learning and dedicate focused practice time to those specific areas. Embrace the difficulties and use them as opportunities for growth and enhanced learning.

Idea 4: Emphasize Elaboration for Deeper Understanding

Elaboration involves going beyond surface-level comprehension by actively seeking connections and explanations for the information you’re learning. By asking “why” and “how” questions and by relating new information to existing knowledge, you deepen your understanding and enhance long-term retention.

Engaging in elaborative activities, such as summarizing concepts in your own words, teaching someone else the material, or generating mental images to represent abstract ideas, promotes meaningful learning. These activities encourage you to actively engage with the information and create stronger memory associations.

Example: When studying historical events, try to understand the underlying causes and consequences, analyze the perspectives of different parties involved, and consider the broader societal implications. This approach will help you develop a richer understanding of the subject matter.

Practical exercise: After studying a new topic or concept, write a brief summary or explanation in your own words. Use analogies or metaphors to relate the information to something familiar, making it easier to remember and comprehend.

Idea 5: Reflect on and Monitor Your Learning

Reflective thinking and metacognition—the awareness and understanding of one’s own thought processes—play crucial roles in effective learning. By regularly reflecting on your learning experiences, evaluating your understanding, and identifying areas for improvement, you can enhance your overall learning outcomes.

Take the time to assess your strengths and weaknesses, identify misconceptions or gaps in your understanding, and actively seek opportunities for growth. Self-monitoring and self-regulation allow you to adjust your study strategies and focus on the areas that require further attention.

Example: After completing a challenging assignment or exam, take a moment to reflect on your performance. Consider what went well, what could have been improved, and what strategies or approaches worked best for you. Use this reflection to inform your future study plans.

Practical exercise: Keep a learning journal where you record your reflections, insights, and areas for growth. Regularly review your journal to track your progress and identify patterns in your learning journey.

Idea 6: Embrace Spacing for Optimal Learning

Spacing refers to the deliberate spacing out of learning sessions over time as opposed to cramming information all at once. Spaced practice enhances long-term retention by allowing for better consolidation of memories and preventing the “forgetting curve” associated with massed practice.

To make the most of spacing, distribute your study sessions across several days or weeks, ensuring you revisit the material at regular intervals. This approach allows for repeated retrieval practice and strengthens memory associations over time.

Example: When preparing for an exam, create a study schedule that includes regular review sessions for each topic or chapter. Instead of cramming all the material into a single marathon study session, space out your study sessions to enhance retention.

Practical exercise: When learning a new skill or subject, break it down into smaller chunks and dedicate separate study sessions to each chunk. Space out these study sessions over time, revisiting the material periodically to reinforce your learning.

Idea 7: Foster a Growth Mindset

Developing a growth mindset is crucial for effective learning. A growth mindset involves believing that intelligence and abilities can be developed through effort and practice, as opposed to a fixed mindset, which assumes that intelligence and abilities are fixed traits.

By embracing a growth mindset, you view challenges as opportunities for growth and learning. You are more likely to persevere through setbacks, embrace feedback, and adopt effective learning strategies. Cultivating a growth mindset creates a positive and resilient attitude towards learning.

Example: Instead of viewing a poor grade or mistake as a reflection of your intelligence or ability, see it as an indication of an area that requires more effort and practice. Embrace the opportunity to learn from the experience and improve.

Practical exercise: Challenge any negative self-talk or limiting beliefs about your abilities. Replace them with positive and growth-oriented affirmations. Remind yourself that learning is a journey, and effort and perseverance lead to growth and improvement.

Idea 8: Harness the Power of Mental Retrieval

Mental retrieval, also known as mental rehearsal or visualization, involves mentally simulating a future performance or recalling information without external aids. Engaging in mental retrieval primes your brain for successful performance and strengthens memory associations.

Visualize yourself successfully executing a task, solving a problem, or recalling information. By vividly imagining the process and outcome, you activate similar brain regions as when physically performing the task, leading to improved performance and memory.

Example: Before giving a presentation, take a few moments to mentally rehearse the key points, visualizing yourself delivering the presentation confidently and engaging the audience.

Practical exercise: Close your eyes and mentally recall the main concepts or information you have been studying. Try to visualize the material as vividly as possible, focusing on the details. This mental retrieval practice enhances memory retrieval and prepares you for future recall.

Idea 9: Tap into the Power of Sleep

Sleep plays a vital role in consolidating memories and enhancing learning. During sleep, the brain processes and strengthens recently acquired information, solidifying memory traces. Sufficiently good sleep ensures optimal learning and retention.

To maximize the benefits of sleep for learning, establish a consistent sleep routine, prioritize the recommended amount of sleep for your age group, and avoid sleep deprivation. By giving your brain adequate time to process and consolidate information, you enhance your learning outcomes.

Example: Before an important exam or presentation, prioritize a good night’s sleep rather than pulling an all-nighter. A well-rested brain performs better and retrieves information more efficiently.

Practical exercise: Create a sleep schedule that allows for the recommended amount of sleep per night. Prioritize a relaxing pre-sleep routine and create a sleep-friendly environment, minimizing distractions and optimizing your sleep quality.

Idea 10: Apply the Principles of Learning to Real-Life Situations

The principles of effective learning discussed in the book extend beyond academic contexts. You can apply these principles to various real-life situations, such as professional development, acquiring new skills, or even personal growth.

By embracing active learning, retrieval practice, spacing, and other evidence-based strategies, you can enhance your ability to learn, adapt, and succeed in different domains of life. The principles of effective learning are transferable and can be applied to diverse learning experiences.

Example: When learning a new hobby or skill, apply the principles of retrieval practice, elaboration, and desirable difficulties. Actively engage with the material, challenge yourself, and reflect on your progress.

Practical exercise: Identify a real-life situation or skill you want to develop and apply the principles of effective learning discussed in the book. Design a learning plan that incorporates active learning strategies and deliberate practice to enhance your learning outcomes.

Who is this Book Recommended For?

“Make It Stick: The Science of Successful Learning” is recommended for learners of all ages and backgrounds who are interested in understanding how to optimize their learning and retention. Students, educators, self-directed learners, and individuals seeking to improve their study habits and enhance their long-term memory will find this book valuable.

This book is particularly relevant for students at all levels, including high school, college, and beyond, as it challenges conventional study methods and provides evidence-based strategies to improve learning outcomes. Educators and teachers can also benefit from the book’s insights to create more effective teaching approaches and help their students achieve deeper understanding and retention.

Moreover, professionals engaged in lifelong learning, such as those pursuing continuing education or seeking to acquire new skills, can gain valuable insights from the book. The principles and techniques discussed can be applied to various professional contexts to enhance learning, problem-solving, and skill development.

Review of the Book and Conclusion

“Make It Stick: The Science of Successful Learning” is a comprehensive and well-researched book that presents evidence-based strategies for effective learning. The authors successfully challenge common misconceptions about learning and provide practical techniques grounded in cognitive psychology and neuroscience.

The book’s emphasis on retrieval practice, spacing, interleaving, and other principles of effective learning encourages readers to adopt active learning strategies and embrace desirable difficulties. The use of real-world examples and practical exercises enhances the book’s accessibility and applicability.

One aspect that could be improved is the organization and structure of the book. The information is sometimes repetitive and could benefit from a more concise presentation. And while the book provides a wealth of scientific research, it may be overwhelming for readers seeking a more streamlined approach.

In short, “Make It Stick” is an important asset for those interested in enhancing their learning and retention abilities. By embracing the principles and strategies outlined in the book, readers can revolutionize their study habits, deepen their understanding, and optimize their long-term retention of knowledge.

This book has the potential to have a significant impact on individuals’ learning journeys and contribute to more effective educational practices in contemporary society.
